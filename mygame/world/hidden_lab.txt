roomzone handle table/restraints = The leather is soft in a way that new leather is not — worn in, the surface almost skin-like, the buckle teeth smooth from having been engaged thousands of times in the same holes. The restraints do not require force to apply; they are designed for compliance and for its opposite in equal measure. The throat band sits across your fingers like a question. The wrist cuffs are sized for a specific wrist — not yours, or maybe exactly yours.

roomzone handle table/instruments = The instruments on the side table are arranged in a line so precise it reads as ritual. You do not touch them. The scalpel's handle catches the orange light from the sconces and holds it; the blade has been honed recently and the edge is not visible so much as implied. The forceps are warm from sitting under the lights — the only warm metal in the room. Picking them up and setting them back down in the wrong position feels, somehow, inadvisable.

roomzone handle cross/cuffs = The cuffs at wrist height open easily — the design is meant to be applied quickly — and close around your wrist with a firmness that is not quite tight and not quite comfortable. The quick-release tab is exactly where they told you it would be, and working it one-handed requires a specific configuration of the fingers that is harder than it should be. The D-ring at the wrist is cold steel and does not shift when you test it. You test it twice. The timber at your back is solid in the way that things built to receive force are solid: not decorating solidity, actually solid.

roomzone handle pillory/wood = The apertures are smooth against your neck and wrists — the wood worn to something closer to stone than timber, the grain polished away by the particular friction of skin over years. When the upper board comes down, the fit is close without being painful, and the pin drops into place with a sound that carries no ambiguity. Your hands hang below the board, palms forward, the position not uncomfortable so much as absolute. The room looks different from this height and this angle. The mirror is directly in your line of sight.

roomzone handle rack/crank = The crank moves smoothly — the ratchet clicks in a slow, deliberate rhythm, and the gauge needle climbs one increment per three turns of the handle. You can feel the tension in the mechanism before you can see it change. The fleece in the shackles compresses with a faint sound when the track takes up slack. At four on the gauge, the pull is insistent. At five, it is a conversation. The safety pin sits in its socket, and you note where it is.

roomzone handle shelves/whips = The single-tail comes off its hook with a weight that clarifies its purpose immediately — the balance is toward the handle, the fall coiled with the muscle memory of someone who knows how to lay it down. You hold it for a moment without moving. The flogger's falls fan open under their own weight, the leather soft and split at the tips in the way of something well-used. Each implement on this wall is what it is without apology. You return it to its hook. The tag swings twice, then settles.

roomzone handle mirror/dimmer = The dimmer moves under your thumb with immediate response — the cell's single bulb brightens by slow degrees, and the shapes beyond the glass resolve: the mat, the trough, the clip-anchor on the wall. At full travel — minus the tape stop — the light in the cell is adequate but not generous. Dim enough for shadow, bright enough to see what the shadow falls on. You press the monitor input on the sound panel and the room's silence in the cell comes through the speaker as a specific, occupied silence, the kind a space holds when it has been recently used and not yet emptied of what happened there.
